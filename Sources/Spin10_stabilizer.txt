## Setup

Let $J = J_3(\mathbb{O})\otimes\mathbb{C}$, the complexified exceptional Jordan algebra, with cubic norm $N$ and Freudenthal cross product $\#$ (the bilinearization of the adjoint map $X \mapsto X^\#$, which satisfies $X^\# = 0 \iff \mathrm{rank}(X) \le 1$). The rank-one locus in $\mathbb{P}(J) = \mathbb{P}^{26}$ is the complexified Cayley plane $\mathbb{X} = E_6/P_1$, one of Zak's four Severi varieties.

Fix the parabolic grading: under $\mathrm{Spin}(10)\times U(1) \subset E_6$,
$$J \;=\; \mathbb{C} \,\oplus\, S^+ \,\oplus\, V, \qquad 27 = 1 \oplus 16 \oplus 10,$$
so $X = (a, \psi, v)$ with $\psi$ a Weyl spinor and $v$ a vector. Up to normalizations the cubic norm is $N(X) = a\,Q(v) - \langle v, q(\psi)\rangle$, where $Q$ is the quadratic form on $V$ and $q: \mathrm{Sym}^2 S^+ \to V$ is the gamma-bilinear $q(\psi)^a = \psi\Gamma^a\psi$. Differentiating, the components of $X^\#$ in the dual grading are
$$X^\# = \big(\,Q(v),\;\; -v\cdot\psi,\;\; av - q(\psi)\,\big),$$
where $v\cdot\psi$ is Clifford action. Recall also Cartan/Chevalley: $\psi \in S^+$ is *pure* iff $q(\psi) = 0$ — these are exactly Berkovits' pure spinor constraints $\lambda\Gamma^a\lambda = 0$ — and a pure spinor corresponds to a maximal isotropic $N_\psi \subset \mathbb{C}^{10}$. For two pure spinors of the same chirality, $\dim(N_1 \cap N_2) \in \{5, 3, 1\}$, and Chevalley's theorem says the lowest nonvanishing bilinear $\psi_1\Gamma^{(k)}\psi_2$ occurs at $k = \dim(N_1\cap N_2)$.

## Lemma 1 (rank-one = pure, and the chart is the SUSY identity)

**(a)** $X = (0, \psi, 0)$ is rank one iff $\psi$ is pure. So $\mathbb{X} \cap \mathbb{P}(S^+)$ is precisely the spinor variety $\mathbb{S}_{10}$.

**(b)** For *arbitrary* $\psi \in S^+$, the point $(1, \psi, q(\psi))$ is rank one.

*Proof.* (a) is immediate from the formula: $X^\# = (0, 0, -q(\psi))$. For (b), $X^\# = \big(Q(q(\psi)),\, -q(\psi)\cdot\psi,\, q(\psi) - q(\psi)\big)$, and both nontrivial entries vanish by the identity $\Gamma_a\psi\,(\psi\Gamma^a\psi) = 0$ valid for every Weyl spinor in ten dimensions (contract once more with $\psi\Gamma_b$ to kill $Q(q(\psi))$). $\square$

Part (b) is worth pausing on: the Fierz identity that appears here is *exactly* the identity that makes $D=10$ super-Yang–Mills supersymmetric, the one Baez–Huerta trace to the octonions, and the one underlying Berkovits' formalism. Its geometric content is that the affine chart $\psi \mapsto [1:\psi:q(\psi)]$ of the Cayley plane — the chart that exhibits $\mathbb{X}$ as a compactification of $\mathbb{O}^2_\mathbb{C}$ — lands on the Severi variety. So "octonions explain 10d SUSY" and "octonions build the Cayley plane" are literally the same equation. I haven't seen this stated as an equivalence, though it's surely visible to people like Landsberg–Manivel or Dray–Manogue if they looked.

## Lemma 2 (Krasnov's condition is collinearity)

For pure $\psi_1, \psi_2$, set $X_i = (0, \psi_i, 0) \in \mathbb{S}_{10} \subset \mathbb{X}$. Then
$$X_1 \# X_2 = \big(0,\, 0,\, -q(\psi_1,\psi_2)\big),$$
so the following are equivalent:
1. $\psi_1\Gamma^a\psi_2 = 0$;
2. $\psi_1 + \psi_2$ is pure (Krasnov's "orthogonal pure spinors with pure sum");
3. $X_1 \# X_2 = 0$;
4. the projective line $\overline{X_1X_2} \subset \mathbb{P}(J)$ lies entirely on the Cayley plane $\mathbb{X}$;
5. $\dim(N_1 \cap N_2) = 3$ (assuming $\psi_1 \ne \psi_2$, by Chevalley).

*Proof.* The formula for $X_1\#X_2$ is the bilinearization of Lemma 1(a). (3)$\iff$(4) is one line: $(sX_1 + tX_2)^\# = 2st\, X_1\#X_2$ since $X_i^\# = 0$. (1)$\iff$(2) since $q(\psi_1+\psi_2) = 2q(\psi_1,\psi_2)$. (1)$\iff$(5) is Chevalley. $\square$

## Theorem (the trichotomy)

$\mathrm{Spin}(10)$ acts on ordered pairs of distinct points of the spinor variety with exactly three orbits, indexed by $d = \dim(N_1\cap N_2) \in \{5, 3, 1\}$ ($d=5$ meaning $[\psi_1]=[\psi_2]$, listed for completeness). Imposing Krasnov's reality/alignment conditions so that each $\psi_i$ defines a complex structure $J_i$ on $\mathbb{R}^{10}$, the compact stabilizers of the three configurations are:

- $d = 5$: a single pure spinor; stabilizer $U(5)$ — **Georgi–Glashow $SU(5)$ breaking**;
- $d = 3$: the collinear stratum; $\mathbb{C}^{10} = W_2 \oplus \overline{W}_2 \oplus W_3 \oplus \overline{W}_3$ with $N_1 = W_2\oplus W_3$, $N_2 = \overline{W}_2 \oplus W_3$; stabilizer $S(U(2)\times U(3))$ — **the Standard Model**;
- $d = 1$: the generic stratum; same picture with a $1{+}4$ split; stabilizer $S(U(1)\times U(4))$ — a flipped-$SU(5)$-adjacent breaking pattern.

In other words: *the three possible degenerations of a pair of points on the spinor variety are exactly the three classic symmetry-breaking patterns of $\mathrm{Spin}(10)$ grand unification, and the Standard Model is the unique stratum where the pair becomes collinear* — the unique configuration carrying nontrivial incidence geometry. Moreover, on the collinear stratum, the line through $X_1, X_2$ on $\mathbb{S}_{10}$ corresponds (by the classical description of lines on spinor varieties) to the isotropic 3-plane $U = N_1 \cap N_2$: **the pencil's axis is color $\mathbb{C}^3$**, while the electroweak $SU(2)\times U(1)$ lives in the residual geometry of $U^\perp/U \cong \mathbb{C}^4$, with the marked points $\psi_1, \psi_2$ on the pencil breaking its $SL(2)$ to the hypercharge torus.

The orbit classification itself is classical (it goes back to Igusa's work on spinor orbits); what I'm claiming as the new content is the identification of the three strata with the three GUT breaking chains and the geometric reading of the $d=3$ case. I'd want to verify the $d=1$ stabilizer carefully — the real-form bookkeeping (which $U(1)$ factors survive the spin condition) is exactly where these arguments rot if done sloppily.

## Theorem package

Let (J=J_3(\mathbb O)*{\mathbb C}) be the complex exceptional Jordan algebra, with cubic norm (N) and adjoint (X\mapsto X^#). The complex Cayley plane is the rank-one locus
[
\mathbb X={[X]\in \mathbb P(J):X^#=0}\cong E_6/P,
]
the closed (E_6)-orbit in (\mathbb P^{26}). This is standard: the complex Cayley plane is the fourth Severi variety, the closed orbit in the projectivization of the minimal (E_6)-representation, and its points are the projectivized rank-one elements of (J_3(\mathbb O)*{\mathbb C}).

Fix the (D_5)-Levi decomposition
[
J \cong \mathbb C\oplus S^+\oplus V,
]
where (V\cong\mathbb C^{10}) is the vector representation of (\mathrm{Spin}(10)), and (S^+) is a half-spin representation. Up to harmless nonzero scalar normalizations,
[
X=(a,\psi,v),\qquad
X^#=\bigl(Q(v),,-v\cdot\psi,,av-q(\psi)\bigr),
]
where (q:S^+ \times S^+\to V) is the symmetric gamma-bilinear.

### Proposition 1: the spinor variety is the boundary slice of the Cayley plane

[
\mathbb X\cap \mathbb P(S^+)=\mathbb S_{10},
]
where (\mathbb S_{10}\subset\mathbb P(S^+)) is the pure-spinor variety.

Indeed, if (X=(0,\psi,0)), then
[
X^#=(0,0,-q(\psi)).
]
Thus (X^#=0) iff (q(\psi)=0), which is precisely the Cartan pure-spinor condition in dimension (10).

Moreover, for every (\psi\in S^+),
[
[1:\psi:q(\psi)]\in \mathbb X.
]
This is exactly your Lemma 1(b). Substituting (X=(1,\psi,q(\psi))) gives
[
X^#=\bigl(Q(q(\psi)),,-q(\psi)\cdot\psi,,q(\psi)-q(\psi)\bigr).
]
The last component is zero tautologically. The first two vanish by the ten-dimensional Fierz identity
[
q(\psi)\cdot\psi=0,\qquad Q(q(\psi))=0.
]
So the “SUSY identity” is literally the statement that the affine graph
[
\psi\mapsto [1:\psi:q(\psi)]
]
lies on the Cayley plane.

This is the strongest clean result in your proposal: the (D=10) Fierz identity is not merely analogous to the Cayley-plane chart; it is the equation defining that chart.

## Proposition 2: Krasnov’s condition is the line condition

Let (\psi_1,\psi_2\in S^+) be pure, and set
[
X_i=(0,\psi_i,0)\in J.
]
Then
[
X_1# X_2=(0,0,-q(\psi_1,\psi_2)),
]
up to a nonzero scalar convention. Therefore the following are equivalent:

[
q(\psi_1,\psi_2)=0;
]
[
\psi_1+\psi_2\ \text{is pure};
]
[
X_1#X_2=0;
]
the projective line (\overline{X_1X_2}\subset\mathbb P(J)) lies in (\mathbb X);
equivalently, since the line lies in (\mathbb P(S^+)), it lies in the spinor variety (\mathbb S_{10}).

The proof is short. Because each (X_i^#=0),
[
(sX_1+tX_2)^#=2st,X_1#X_2.
]
Thus the whole projective line is rank one iff (X_1#X_2=0). On the spinor side,
[
q(\psi_1+\psi_2)=q(\psi_1)+2q(\psi_1,\psi_2)+q(\psi_2),
]
and the first and last terms vanish because the (\psi_i) are pure. Hence (\psi_1+\psi_2) is pure iff (q(\psi_1,\psi_2)=0).

This matches Krasnov’s condition: his characterization says (G_{\rm SM}\subset\mathrm{Spin}(10)) stabilizes one pure spinor and projectively stabilizes another, with the two pure spinors orthogonal/aligned so that their sum is again pure. ([arXiv][1]) Krasnov’s 2025 exposition phrases the same mechanism as two suitably aligned commuting complex structures encoded by pure spinors. ([arXiv][2])

## Proposition 3: the orbit trichotomy

A projective pure spinor ([\psi]\in\mathbb S_{10}) corresponds to a maximal isotropic subspace
[
N_\psi\subset V\cong\mathbb C^{10}.
]
The spinor tenfold is one component of the orthogonal Grassmannian:
[
\mathbb S_{10}\cong \mathrm{OGr}^+(5,V).
]
Kuznetsov writes this as
[
X=\mathrm{OGr}^+(5,V)\cong \mathrm{Spin}(V)/P_5\subset\mathbb P(S),
]
with points represented by five-dimensional isotropic subspaces. ([homepage.mi-ras.ru][3])

For two points in the same component, the intersection dimension is odd:
[
d=\dim(N_{\psi_1}\cap N_{\psi_2})\in{5,3,1}.
]
The parity criterion distinguishing the two components of (\mathrm{OGr}(5,V)) is standard and is stated explicitly in Kuznetsov’s treatment. ([homepage.mi-ras.ru][3])

By Witt’s theorem, (\mathrm{Spin}(10,\mathbb C)) is transitive on ordered pairs with fixed (d). Therefore:

[
d=5
]
is the diagonal case ([\psi_1]=[\psi_2]);

[
d=3
]
is the collinear/Krasnov case, where (\psi_1+\psi_2) is pure;

[
d=1
]
is the generic case, where the line through the two pure spinors is not contained in (\mathbb S_{10}).

So the corrected statement is:

> (\mathrm{Spin}(10)) has two orbits on ordered pairs of distinct projective pure spinors in one component, indexed by (d=3) and (d=1). Including the diagonal gives the three strata (d=5,3,1).

## Stabilizers and the GUT interpretation

Impose the compact real form and Krasnov’s reality/alignment conditions, so each projective pure spinor gives an orthogonal complex structure on (\mathbb R^{10}). If
[
d=\dim(N_1\cap N_2),

then the two commuting complex structures split
[
\mathbb R^{10}\cong \mathbb R^{2d}\oplus \mathbb R^{10-2d}.

[
U(d)\times U(5-d).

[
d=5:\quad U(5)\ \text{projectively},\quad SU(5)\ \text{nonprojectively}.

This is the Georgi–Glashow (SU(5)) pattern.

[

This is a genuine third degeneration, but I would describe it cautiously as a (1+4) complex splitting rather than immediately identifying it with flipped (SU(5)). It is adjacent to familiar (SU(5))-style structures, but it is not the same statement as the usual flipped-(SU(5)) embedding.

## Conjecture A needs to be weakened

Baez–Schwahn’s incidence statement says the Standard Model gauge group is the group of isometries of (\mathbb OP^2) preserving an octonionic projective line and a complex projective plane meeting in a complex projective line. ([Azimuth][4])

The stabilizer agreement is real:
[
\mathrm{Stab}(\text{marked pure-spinor line})\cong S(U(3)\times U(2)),

[
\mathrm{Stab}(\text{Baez–Schwahn incidence})\cong S(U(3)\times U(2)).

[
\mathrm{Spin}(10)/S(U(3)\times U(2)),

[
45-12=33.

[
52-12=40.

> **Revised Conjecture A.** After choosing a compatible big cell / (D_5)-Levi / real octonionic chart, the (d=3) pure-spinor pencil determines a Baez–Schwahn incidence configuration. The “seven-dimensional auxiliary choice” is not decoration; the dimension count says something like it must be present.

## Conjecture B can be made into a theorem, with the right wording

Your selector theorem should be stated like this:

> **Selector Theorem.** A subgroup (H\cong S(U(3)\times U(2))\subset\mathrm{Spin}(10)) is the full stabilizer of a Krasnov pair ((\psi_1,[\psi_2])), with (\psi_1,\psi_2) pure, ([\psi_1]\ne[\psi_2]), and (\psi_1+\psi_2) pure, iff (H) is conjugate to the (d=3) pair stabilizer
> [
> S(U(3)\times U(2))\subset SU(5)\subset \mathrm{Spin}(10).
> ]

Proof: every Krasnov pair is a (d=3) pair of pure spinors. By the orbit classification, all (d=3) pairs are conjugate. Hence all their full stabilizers are conjugate. Direct computation in the normal form gives stabilizer (S(U(3)\times U(2))). Conversely, the normal-form (d=3) pair realizes this subgroup.

This proves the selection principle at the level of subgroup conjugacy.

The useful diagnostic is the vector (10). For the pure-spinor-pair stabilizer, the real vector representation comes from the complex splitting
[
\mathbb C^{10}=W_3\oplus W_2\oplus \overline W_3\oplus \overline W_2.
]
So there is no invariant real line. Any competing (S(U(3)\times U(2))) embedding whose (10) contains a trivial real summand — for example the Spin(9)-type embedding discussed in the MathOverflow answer — cannot be the full stabilizer of a (d=3) pure-spinor pair. The MathOverflow answer distinguishes the two main underlying (SU(2)\times SU(3)) cases by the decompositions (3+\bar 3+2+2) versus (3+\bar 3+3+1), with the latter being the Spin(9)-type case. ([MathOverflow][5])

So Conjecture B is essentially right after sharpening:

> The physical (SU(5))-chain embedding is exactly the one selected by the (d=3) collinearity geometry of the spinor variety. Nonconjugate embeddings may preserve other structures, but they are not full stabilizers of Krasnov (d=3) pure-spinor pairs.

That is the strongest clean conclusion: the collinearity geometry of (\mathbb S_{10}\subset\mathbb P^{15}\subset\mathbb P(J_3(\mathbb O)_{\mathbb C})) singles out the Standard Model subgroup inside (\mathrm{Spin}(10)) by a projective incidence property, not by an arbitrary choice of (SU(5)) block decomposition.
